DATA VISUALIZATION

FINANCIAL PLANNING VISUALIZATION

PROJECT CONTEXT

This visualization was created for my Fintech Finance Course at SIT, demonstrating the long-term impact of compound interest on Singapore’s Central Provident Fund (CPF) savings.

THE VISUALIZATION

WHAT WAS VISUALIZED

X-AXIS

Age in years (25 to 65) - continuous numeric variable representing career progression

Y-AXIS

Account balance in SGD - monetary values showing accumulated savings over time

THREE SERIES

Ordinary, Special, MediSave Accounts - each representing different CPF account types with distinct interest rates

DATA SOURCE AND ASSUMPTIONS

OFFICIAL RATES

— CPF contribution rates from CPF Board (2024)

— Interest rates: OA (2.5% p.a.), SA (4% p.a.), MA (4% p.a.)

— Based on actual Singapore government policies

CALCULATION ASSUMPTIONS

— Starting monthly salary: SGD $3,500

— Annual salary increment: 3%

— Contribution percentages: OA (23%), SA (16%), MA (8%)

— Time horizon: 40 years (age 25-65)

— No voluntary contributions or withdrawals

RATIONALE FOR VISUALIZATION METHOD

WHY LINE CHART?

I chose a line chart because it’s the optimal choice for displaying time-series financial data with multiple comparisons.

ADVANTAGES OF LINE CHART

Time-series excellence: Shows how numeric values change over continuous time periods

Trend visibility: Upward curves clearly show exponential growth - steeper slopes in later years are immediately visible

Multiple comparisons: Three distinct colors enable easy comparison of different account growth rates

Financial convention: Standard for investment growth visualization, making interpretation intuitive

WHY NOT ALTERNATIVES

Bar chart: Would create visual clutter with 40+ bars per account type

Stacked area chart: Makes it difficult to read individual account values accurately

Scatter plot: Would suggest discrete data points rather than continuous progression

Pie chart: Cannot show changes over time

KEY INSIGHT DISCOVERED

The visualization revealed something surprising: the last 10 years of savings (age 55-65) contribute almost as much absolute value as the first 20 years (age 25-45).

Why? Compound interest. By year 30, you’re earning interest on decades of contributions AND their accumulated interest. The raw numbers showed this, but the upward curving lines made it intuitively clear.

KEY INSIGHTS FROM THE DATA

COMPOUND INTEREST POWER

Age 30 (5 years): Total ≈ $115,000

Age 50 (25 years): Total ≈ $800,000

INSIGHT: The middle 20 years contribute 7x more than the first 5 years

ACCOUNT TYPE DIFFERENCES

Special Account: 4% interest

Ordinary Account: 2.5% interest

TAKEAWAY: This 1.5% difference creates over $80,000 additional value over 40 years

EARLY VS LATE CONTRIBUTIONS

$1,000 at age 25: grows to ~$2,685 (2.69x)

$1,000 at age 45: grows to ~$1,480 (1.48x)

CONCLUSION: Starting early provides 82% more growth per dollar invested

CONCLUSION

Early contributions have disproportionate impact - visible in curve accumulation patterns

Higher interest accounts grow significantly faster - visible in progressive line separation

Growth accelerates exponentially over time - visible in steepening curves toward retirement age

The key achievement: converting hundreds of individual numeric calculations into a single comprehensible story about the mathematical power of compound interest and the importance of early financial planning.
